01

What academic writing actually is

Features of academic writing

Academic writing is simply the skill of explaining and defending your own ideas about a topic in a way other students or researchers can follow and check. It covers essays, term papers, theses, articles, and even full books (monographs) — basically any text where you're expected to think for yourself and back it up.

Three things separate it from ordinary writing. Every claim needs evidence — you don't just say "this is true," you show where that comes from. The structure is deliberate — intro, body, conclusion, not a stream of thoughts. And the language is precise and formal rather than casual or emotional.

Your course notes specifically push back on one common confusion: people often think "academic writing skill" just means good spelling and grammar. Spelling matters, but it's the smallest part of it — the real skill is organizing and arguing ideas clearly, which is exactly what neither school nor most university courses ever teach directly.

Types of academic writing

Different academic genres do different jobs, so knowing which is which helps. An essay argues a personal position, usually in one of three modes: persuasion (convince the reader of a viewpoint), narration (tell a sequence of events), or reasoning (explain causes and effects, or how something works).

An abstract / annotation is a short, compressed summary of someone else's longer work. A term paper or thesis is a full structured research project written for a course or degree. A scientific article reports an original result for a journal. A monograph is a complete book on one topic by one or a few authors. And a review isn't a new discovery at all — it's an evaluation of research that already exists.

Structure of academic writing & its planningalso: "scientific research requires…", "the plot of scientific work"

Writing anything substantial — a term paper, a thesis — isn't one step, it's a planned sequence. Your notes break it into roughly seven real stages.

  1. Preparatory — pick and get your topic, plan, and hypothesis approved.
  2. Studying what's already known — collect and organize the literature, look through archives.
  3. Draft writing — decide your chapters and headings, write a full first version, adjust the plan as you go.
  4. Editing — fix the manuscript, format the reference list and appendices, proofread.
  5. Final formatting — match the official requirements, hand it to your supervisor.
  6. Defense prep — build your presentation and your speech.
  7. Defense — present, usually 10 to 15 minutes.
!

Watch outYour slides number this list up to "8," but step 8 is actually the start of a brand new topic (sources of information), not a real eighth stage. So if you only count seven real planning steps, you haven't missed anything.

Main parts of academic writing

Separate from the planning stages above, every finished academic text — paper, thesis, article — tends to follow the same skeleton of parts, whatever the subject. A specific Title that tells the reader exactly what's inside. An Abstract, a short stand-alone summary covering the problem, the approach and the main result, written so someone could understand your whole project from that one paragraph alone. An Introduction explaining why the topic matters and what question you're answering. A Main body where you review what's already known, explain your method, and present your findings. A Conclusion that says what you found and why it matters. And a References list of everything you cited.

Think of the seven planning stages as how you build the house, and these main parts as what rooms the finished house actually has.

02

Working with sources

References & citations — the basics

A citation is a marker that says "this idea or these words came from somewhere else, here's where." There are two honest ways to use someone else's material. A direct quotation copies their exact words inside quotation marks, and you also note the page where it starts. An indirect citation takes their idea and puts it into your own words — you still point to where it came from, you just don't use their exact wording.

One small but easy-to-miss rule: when you quote directly, quote a complete chunk — a whole sentence or a whole short paragraph, not a random fragment — and if the quoted chunk is a full sentence, punctuate it like direct speech.

There's also a difference between a citation and a plain "extract" you make for yourself while reading: an extract is just your own working notes in your own words, while a direct quotation has to preserve the author's exact wording, because the wording itself carries meaning. That matters a lot for legal texts and historical documents, which must be reproduced with full accuracy — exact title, year, publisher, even the archive where the original is kept.

Functions of referencing

Why bother citing anything at all? Four honest reasons. It gives credit — the difference between presenting an idea as yours versus honestly showing it's someone else's, which is exactly what keeps you out of plagiarism trouble. It lets your reader check you — they can go look at your source and confirm you represented it fairly. It strengthens your argument — showing you've engaged with existing research makes your conclusions more convincing than if you just asserted them. And it connects research over time — your reference list is literally how one researcher's work leads another researcher to it years later, which is how a whole field builds on itself instead of everyone starting from zero.

Criteria for selecting sources

Not every source you find deserves to end up in your paper. A simple way to test one — often remembered as the CRAAP check — is to ask five questions. Is it current, or has something newer replaced it? Is it relevant — does it answer your specific question, not just mention your topic in passing? Does it have authority — written by someone qualified, published somewhere credible, not posted anonymously online? Is it accurate — can you verify its facts elsewhere? And what's its purpose — written to inform honestly, or to sell, persuade, or push an agenda?

If a source fails most of these, leave it out, no matter how easy it was to find.

Primary vs secondary scientific information

Sources of scientific information split into two layers. Primary sources are the original, first-hand output of research — monographs, collections of scientific papers, dissertation abstracts, raw research reports. Think of this as straight from the researcher's own desk.

Secondary sources are built by processing primary sources for you — reference books, abstract or review publications, bibliographies. Think of this as someone else having already read a pile of primary sources and organized or summarized them so you don't have to start from scratch.

★

Memory tipA primary source is the actual recipe a chef wrote down. A secondary source is a restaurant guide that's already read and rated hundreds of chefs' recipes for you.

03

Tools & citation styles

Referencing tools — Mendeley, EndNote, BibTeX

These are tools that store your sources for you, so you don't have to type out a reference list by hand and risk getting the formatting wrong.

Mendeley is free, friendly, and includes a built-in PDF reader and annotator; it works on desktop, in the browser, and on your phone — a good first reference manager for a student. EndNote is the more powerful, paid option: better for managing huge libraries of hundreds of sources, more customization, and it can auto-fill missing reference details from academic databases. BibTeX is different from the other two — it's not really an app, it's a plain-text file format used with LaTeX (a typesetting system common in maths, physics and engineering papers). You keep your sources as a simple list of tagged entries, and the citation style is generated automatically when the document compiles.

★

Memory tipMendeley and EndNote are full programs with a library and a Word plugin. BibTeX is just a reference file format for LaTeX users.

Reference styles — APA, Harvard, MLA, IEEE

Different academic fields settled on different citation formats, and the differences aren't random — each style grew out of what that field actually needs to see at a glance.

StyleMostly used inIn-text citation looks likeEnd list is called
APASocial sciences, education, psychology(Smith, 2020)References
HarvardGeneral / UK & Australia, business(Smith, 2020) — varies a bit by university, no single official rulebookReference list
MLAHumanities, literature(Smith 45) — page number, no yearWorks Cited

Memory hook: APA and Harvard care about WHEN something was published, because newer research often replaces older research in those fields. MLA cares about WHERE — the exact page — because literature scholars are often analysing a precise line of text. IEEE cares about ORDER — a running number — because technical papers cite dozens of sources quickly and a number reads faster than a name.

!

Watch outIEEE uses a number inside square brackets, like [1], directly in the sentence — not a small floating footnote number above the line. Mixing these two up is an easy point to lose.

04

Using someone else's words without copying them

Paraphrasing & expressing ideas in your own words

Paraphrasing means rewriting somebody's idea using different words and a different sentence shape, while keeping the same meaning — and you still cite where the idea came from, you just don't put it in quotation marks.

The single most common mistake is thinking paraphrasing just means swapping a few words for synonyms. It doesn't — that still counts as too close to the original. Four techniques actually work, and work best combined:

  1. Swap several key words for genuine synonyms — not just one or two.
  2. Restructure the sentence — active into passive, split one long sentence into two, reorder the clauses.
  3. Change the part of speech — turn a verb into a noun or back ("the team analyzed the data" → "the analysis of the data, carried out by the team, showed…").
  4. Read it, cover it, then explain it like you're talking to a friend — read the original, cover the page, explain the idea from memory, then check back that the meaning survived.

Your lecture notes also classify quoted material this way: a thesis (a short stated point) can be simple — just one statement with nothing backing it up — or complex/detailed, where the statement comes with supporting arguments. It can also be textual, staying close to the source's own wording, or free, where the idea is rebuilt fully in your own words. Free, detailed theses are generally the strongest writing, because they prove you actually understood the source rather than just copied its shape.

Editing academic writing

Editing is stage four of the planning process you saw earlier, but it deserves its own attention as a skill. Do it in three passes, big to small.

First, the big things: does the structure make sense, are the chapters in a logical order, is anything missing or repeated? Second, the medium things: is every claim actually backed by a reference, are quotations formatted correctly, are your headings consistent? Third, the small things: spelling, punctuation, grammar.

Remember the earlier point about "literacy" meaning more than just spelling — that's exactly why editing academic writing needs more than a spellchecker. It needs someone checking that the logic actually flows, and that your in-text citations match what's in your final reference list.

05

Bibliographies & critical reading

Bibliography

People use "bibliography" loosely to mean "the list of sources at the end," but there's a real distinction worth knowing. A reference list contains only sources you actually cited in your text. A bibliography can be broader — everything you read or consulted while researching, even things you never directly quoted.

Different citation styles even use different names for basically the same idea: APA and Harvard call it a "reference list," MLA calls it "Works Cited." In the terms used in this course, a bibliographic publication is an informational publication containing an ordered set of full source descriptions — in plain words, an organized list detailed enough that a reader could go and find every source themselves.

!

Watch outIf asked to define bibliography on the exam, the safe answer is: an ordered, organized list of full source descriptions, so a reader can locate every work that informed the research — not just "a fancy word for reference list."

Critical reading & reviewing an article

Critical reading isn't reading to remember facts — it's reading while constantly questioning the text. What exactly is the author claiming? What evidence backs it up, and is that evidence strong, recent, or possibly biased? What did the author leave out? Do other researchers in the field agree or push back?

"Reviewing" an article turns that critical reading into a short written piece: a brief, fair summary of what the article actually says in your own words, plus your own honest evaluation of its strengths, weaknesses, and how it fits next to everything else you know about the topic. The key difference from a plain summary: a review judges the work, it doesn't just describe it.

06

Review articles

Types of review articlesmini/full · narrative/systematic

A review article doesn't report a brand-new experiment — it gathers, organizes and evaluates everything already published on a topic, so a reader gets a clear, current picture without reading fifty separate papers themselves. They're classified two ways at once: by how much they cover, and by how strictly they're built.

By depth: a mini-review is short, covers a narrow slice of a topic, and uses relatively few sources — a quick, focused overview. A full (comprehensive) review is long, covers a whole field broadly, and draws on many sources — often substantial enough to be a major article on its own.

By method: a narrative review is the classic kind — the author picks and discusses sources based on their own expertise and judgment. It's flexible and especially good at tracing how an idea developed over time, but it's also more subjective. A systematic review follows a strict, written-in-advance plan: one specific question, clear written rules for which studies are allowed in, a documented and thorough search, and a method built so another researcher could repeat it and get the same result. It's far more objective — considered the gold standard, especially in medicine — but it takes much more time and work.

★

Memory tipA narrative review is a guided tour led by an expert who picks the best stops for you. A systematic review is a checklist itinerary that visits every relevant stop in a fixed order, no matter who's leading it.

!

Watch out"Systematic" doesn't just mean "thorough" or "a lot of reading." It specifically means a predefined protocol existed — a question and search rules written down before the search even started. A narrative review can also be thorough and still not count as systematic.

Structure of a review article (components)

A review article's components, in order: Title and abstract (what it covers, and the main takeaway, in brief). Introduction (why this topic now, what's the gap in current understanding). Main body, organized into themed sections rather than one paper-by-paper summary in date order — group findings by theme, compare them against each other, and point out where studies agree or disagree. Discussion/conclusion (what we now know overall, what's still unclear, what should be studied next). References — often a long list, since the whole point of a review is gathering many sources.

Steps for preparing a review article (algorithm)

  1. Check the topic is actually worth reviewing — is it current, and is there enough recent primary research out there?
  2. Search broadly and collect sources using the source-selection criteria covered earlier, ideally organized with a tool like Mendeley.
  3. Read everything critically and group your sources by theme or finding, not by publication date.
  4. Sketch the outline using the structure above.
  5. Write the draft, comparing and contrasting themes instead of listing summaries one after another.
  6. Edit, and finalize the reference list in whichever citation style is required.
07

Presenting research & publication types

Preparing presentations on scientific research

A presentation is not your written text shrunk down onto slides — it needs far less text per slide, one clear idea per slide, and you have to be ready to explain it in your own words instead of reading it off the screen.

The structure mirrors a review article: a short opening on why the topic matters, the key findings grouped by theme, a clear takeaway at the end, and room left for questions. This connects directly back to the defense stage of the planning process covered earlier — preparing your presentation and your speech is literally the second-to-last step before defending any major piece of academic work.

Scientific research: traditional approach vs international experience

Your lecture notes use France as a case study for comparing how seriously, and how early, research-writing skills get taught elsewhere. French universities run a 3+2+3 system — Bachelor's, then Master's, then Doctorate — matching the wider European reform this region is also moving toward.

From the very start of their Master's, French students choose either a research track or a professional track, and can still switch later. Either way, research is treated as central, not extra: students often publish their results as an actual article, or sometimes even a collective textbook written with classmates, and from the second year onward they join a dedicated doctoral school's seminars alongside their regular coursework.

The point of this comparison isn't to memorize French bureaucracy — it's to notice how much variation exists internationally in how early and how formally research-writing gets taught, which is exactly the gap this course is trying to close locally.

Research types at French universitieseducational research · special feature of the magistracy

French master's students carry out research projects that differ by year and by track, and the notes name three specific types. Mémoire d'étude is educational research, done in the first year — this is what the question bank calls "educational research": basic, foundational, sometimes done by a small team rather than solo, and its result is often formalized as an article or even a collective educational monograph. Mémoire de recherche is proper scientific research, done in the second year by students on the research track. Mémoire professionnalisé is professional, practice-based research, also second year, by students on the professional track.

One specific feature worth remembering: not every first-year student automatically continues into year two. Students must first successfully defend their first-year research, and a special committee reviews each student's "dossier" — a file of their overall academic record — before deciding who continues. Only the students who pass this review join the research school directly with a supervisor.
