Lecture 1 · Introduction to Petroleum Recovery Mechanisms
1 What is the role of viscosity in EOR processes? ▾
Viscosity = how thick/resistant a fluid is to flow. In EOR it matters because:
  • High oil viscosity → oil moves slowly and is hard to displace.
  • If injected water is much less viscous than oil, water "fingers" through the reservoir, bypassing oil (bad sweep).
  • The mobility ratio M = mobility of displacing fluid ÷ mobility of oil. If M > 1 → unstable, poor sweep.
How EOR addresses it:
  • Steam/thermal EOR: heat reduces oil viscosity dramatically (e.g., 1000 mPa·s → 20 mPa·s).
  • Polymer flooding: thickens water → lower M → better sweep.
  • CO₂/miscible: mixes with oil, swells it and reduces viscosity.
Key rule: reduce oil viscosity OR increase injected-fluid viscosity to get a better mobility ratio.

4 What are the main criteria for selecting injection and production well patterns? ▾
Key criteria:
  • Reservoir size and geometry — large, elongated reservoirs use line-drive patterns; compact reservoirs use 5-spot.
  • Permeability distribution — heterogeneous reservoirs need injection rows perpendicular to high-perm channels.
  • Oil viscosity — high viscosity → denser well patterns, more injectors per producer.
  • Mobility ratio — unfavorable M → tighter patterns to avoid early water breakthrough.
  • Structural position — up-dip injection exploits gravity for gas; down-dip for water.
Common patterns:
  • 5-spot: 1 injector surrounded by 4 producers (most common).
  • 7-spot: 1 injector, 6 producers.
  • Line drive: rows of injectors and producers alternating.
  • 9-spot: used in heterogeneous or low-permeability reservoirs.
5 Explain the processes of maturation, migration, and accumulation of hydrocarbons. ▾
1. Maturation (source rock → oil/gas):
  • Organic matter (kerogen) buried under sediment gets heated by geothermal gradient.
  • "Oil window": ~60–120°C → liquid oil generated.
  • "Gas window": >120°C → dry gas generated.
  • Measured by Tmax, Ro (vitrinite reflectance), HI.
2. Migration (source → reservoir):
  • Primary migration: expelled from source rock pores (short distance).
  • Secondary migration: moves through carrier beds, faults, fractures until trapped.
  • Driven by buoyancy (oil/gas less dense than water) and pressure.
3. Accumulation (reservoir + trap):
  • Oil/gas fills porous reservoir rock (sandstone, limestone).
  • Held in place by an impermeable cap rock (shale, anhydrite) in a structural or stratigraphic trap.

7 Describe Petroleum System Elements. ▾
Five essential elements — all must be present and in the right timing:
  • Source Rock — organic-rich rock (shale, mudstone) that generates oil/gas when heated.
  • Reservoir Rock — porous and permeable rock that stores hydrocarbons (sandstone, limestone).
  • Seal / Cap Rock — impermeable barrier above reservoir preventing escape (shale, anhydrite).
  • Trap — geometric configuration that concentrates hydrocarbons (anticline, fault, stratigraphic pinch-out).
  • Overburden — overlying rock providing burial depth, heat, and pressure for maturation.
Plus timing: generation must happen after the trap was formed, otherwise oil escapes.

  • Good: Jurassic Limestone (Arabian Peninsula) — high porosity from dissolution (vugs), karst features.
  • Poor: Tight Sandstone — heavily cemented, porosity <10%, permeability <0.1 mD.
  • Poor: Shaley sandstone — clay minerals fill pores, permeability very low.

11 Explain parameters of source rock. ▾
  • TOC — organic richness (see Q10).
  • HI (Hydrogen Index) = S2/TOC × 100 → kerogen type; high HI = oil-prone.
  • OI (Oxygen Index) = S3/TOC × 100 → high OI = gas-prone or immature.
  • PI (Production Index) = S1 / (S1 + S2) → how much potential has already been used; >0.4 = highly mature or migrated.
  • Vitrinite Reflectance (Ro%) — optical maturity: <0.5 = immature; 0.5–1.3 = oil window; >1.3 = gas zone.
  • Tmax — thermal maturity from Rock-Eval (see Q10).
  • Type of kerogen — determines oil vs gas generation potential.

Lecture 5 · Fundamentals of Porosity and Permeability
13 What is Porosity and which parameters affect it. Give source examples both of good and poor. ▾
Porosity (φ) = fraction of rock volume that is void space (pores). Expressed as %.
Formula: φ = Vp / Vt × 100

Parameters that affect porosity:
  • Grain sorting — well-sorted = uniform grain size = higher porosity.
  • Grain shape — angular grains pack more loosely → higher porosity.
  • Cementation — silica, calcite, dolomite cement fills pores → lowers porosity.
  • Compaction — burial depth increases overburden stress, compresses pores.
  • Diagenesis — dissolution can create secondary porosity (vugs); minerals can block pores.
  • Fractures — add fracture porosity.
Examples:
  • ✅ Good: Aeolian (wind-deposited) sandstone — 25–35%; clean, well-sorted.
  • ✅ Good: Vuggy limestone — 20–30% (dissolution porosity).
  • ❌ Poor: Deeply buried, cemented sandstone — <5%.
  • ❌ Poor: Shale — <5% (pores too small, not connected).
14 What is Permeability and which parameters affect it, give source examples both of good and poor. ▾
Permeability (k) = ability of rock to allow fluid to flow through connected pores. Measured in milliDarcies (mD).
Governed by Darcy's Law: Q = kA(ΔP) / (μL)

Parameters affecting permeability:
  • Pore throat size — the bottleneck for flow; small throats = low k even with high porosity.
  • Pore connectivity — disconnected pores = zero permeability.
  • Clay content — swelling clays (smectite) block pore throats.
  • Fractures — dramatically increase permeability in otherwise tight rock.
  • Cementation and compaction — reduce k just like porosity.
Examples:
  • ✅ Good: Coarse clean sandstone — 500–2000 mD.
  • ✅ Good: Naturally fractured carbonate — 10–1000 mD (via fractures).
  • ❌ Poor: Shale — 0.00001–0.001 mD (source rock, not reservoir).
  • ❌ Poor: Tight gas sandstone — <0.1 mD (needs fracking).
15 Explain the relationship between porosity and permeability, and discuss how these properties influence EOR. ▾
Relationship: Generally a positive correlation — higher porosity tends to mean higher permeability. BUT not always:
  • Vuggy carbonates: high porosity but vugs may not be connected → low k.
  • Fractured tight rock: low matrix porosity but high k via fractures.
Influence on EOR:
  • High porosity = more oil in place (OOIP) = more potential.
  • High permeability = easier to inject EOR fluids and get oil out.
  • Heterogeneity in k → injected fluids channel through high-k zones, bypassing low-k zones (poor sweep).
  • Polymer flooding helps when permeability contrast is high (diverts flow).
  • Fractured carbonate → chemical EOR may work poorly (chemicals flow fast through fractures, bypass matrix oil).
EOR success = not just about average properties, but how uniform (homogeneous) they are.
16 Explain how reservoir heterogeneity affects the success of EOR methods. repeat ▾

⚠ Also appears in Lecture 7

Reservoir heterogeneity = variation in rock properties (permeability, porosity, lithology) throughout the reservoir.

Effects on EOR:
  • Thief zones: high-k layers take most of the injected fluid → bypasses oil in low-k zones.
  • Channeling: early breakthrough of injected fluid at producer wells → less contact with oil.
  • Vertical sweep efficiency drops when different layers have very different permeabilities (Dykstra-Parsons coefficient).
How EOR methods deal with it:
  • Polymer flooding: thickens water → reduces flow in high-k zones → better coverage of low-k zones.
  • Gel treatments (conformance control): block high-k zones with crosslinked polymer.
  • Steam flooding: heat evens out over time, but still challenged by heterogeneity.
High heterogeneity = lower EOR efficiency. The more uniform the reservoir, the better the EOR sweep.
17 What is the difference between steady-state and unsteady-state relative permeability measurements? ▾
Relative permeability = permeability to one fluid when multiple fluids coexist in pores (e.g., oil + water). Critical for modeling EOR.

20 What are the main challenges associated with CO₂ injection in reservoirs? ▾
  • Miscibility pressure (MMP): CO₂ only fully mixes with oil above a minimum pressure. Deep enough reservoir needed.
  • Viscous fingering: CO₂ is much less viscous than oil → channels through reservoir, poor sweep.
  • Gravity override: CO₂ is lighter than oil → rises to top, bypasses bottom oil.
  • Asphaltene precipitation: CO₂ mixing can destabilize asphaltenes, plugging pores.
  • Corrosion: CO₂ + water → carbonic acid → corrodes steel equipment.
  • CO₂ supply and cost: Large volumes needed; pipeline infrastructure required.
  • Recycling: Produced CO₂ must be captured and re-injected.
21 What is reservoir boundary conditions? ▾
Boundary conditions define how pressure and fluid behave at the edges of the reservoir in flow models:
  • No-flow (closed) boundary: impermeable barrier (fault, facies pinch-out). Pressure declines as production proceeds — like a sealed box.
  • Constant pressure boundary: connected to an active aquifer or gas cap. Pressure stays roughly constant — reservoir has external support.
  • Mixed boundary: partial support from aquifer (common in real fields).
Why important:
  • Wrong boundary → wrong pressure decline prediction → wrong production forecast.
  • Defines whether water flooding or natural drive is needed.
  • Used in well test analysis (pressure transient) to identify reservoir size and connectivity.

23 What are the main limitations of primary recovery? ▾
  • Limited energy: relies purely on natural pressure — depletes as oil is produced.
  • Pressure below bubble point: gas comes out of solution, oil becomes heavier and less mobile.
  • Capillary forces: trap oil in small pores — can't be overcome without EOR.
  • Low sweep efficiency: natural drive often sweeps only part of the reservoir.
  • Typical recovery only 10–15% of OOIP — most oil is left behind.
  • Declining flow rates: wellbore pressure drop reduces production rate over time.
Lecture 9 · Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR) Techniques
24 What are the main mechanisms of oil recovery in steam flooding? ▾
  • Viscosity reduction (most important): heat drops oil viscosity by orders of magnitude, making it flow easily.
  • Thermal expansion: oil volume expands with heat, pushing oil toward producers.
  • Steam drive (pressure): injected steam pressure physically drives oil.
  • Distillation: light oil components vaporize, travel ahead of steam front, condense near producer — solvent-like effect.
  • Relative permeability improvement: high temperature reduces water viscosity more than oil, slightly improving relative flow.
  • Hot water front: hot condensed water ahead of steam zone also displaces oil.
Best for: heavy oil (>10,000 mPa·s), shallow reservoirs (<1000 m), thick pay zones.
25 Describe the function of surfactants in chemical EOR. ▾
Surfactants (surface-active agents) work at the oil-water interface:
  • Reduce interfacial tension (IFT): normally oil is held in pores by capillary forces. Surfactants reduce IFT from ~30 mN/m down to <0.01 mN/m → capillary number increases → trapped oil is mobilized.
  • Wettability alteration: can change rock surface from oil-wet to water-wet, improving displacement of oil.
  • Emulsification: create oil-in-water emulsions that help transport oil to producers.
Key challenge: adsorption — surfactant molecules stick to rock surfaces, reducing effective concentration. Requires large volumes or pre-flush.
Often used in ASP (Alkaline-Surfactant-Polymer) floods combining wettability change, IFT reduction, and mobility control.
26 What is polymer flooding, and how does it improve oil recovery? ▾
Polymer flooding: add long-chain polymers (usually HPAM – hydrolyzed polyacrylamide) to injection water to make it more viscous.

How it improves recovery:
  • Better mobility ratio: thicker water moves more slowly → doesn't finger through oil as badly.
  • More uniform sweep: viscous water is diverted from high-k thief zones into low-k unswept zones.
  • Delayed water breakthrough: oil produced for longer before water floods producers.
Best for: High-viscosity oil, heterogeneous reservoirs, mature waterflood fields with poor sweep.

Limitations:
  • Polymer degrades at high temperature (>90°C) and high salinity.
  • Can plug formation if too concentrated.
  • More expensive than plain water flooding.

28 How does bacterial activity (biofouling) affect reservoir properties during EOR? ▾
Biofouling: bacteria grow in injection systems and reservoir pores, forming biofilms.

Negative effects:
  • Pore plugging: biofilm reduces pore throat size → lower permeability → reduced injectivity.
  • Reservoir souring: sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB) convert sulfate → H₂S → toxic, corrosive, lowers oil value.
  • Corrosion (MIC): bacteria produce acids and H₂S → attack steel pipes and equipment.
  • Increased maintenance: more workovers, filter replacements, corrosion monitoring.
Some positive use (MEOR): controlled bacterial injection can plug high-k thief zones or produce biosurfactants.
Control: biocides (glutaraldehyde, quaternary ammonium), regular pigging, UV treatment.

30 How does microbial-induced corrosion (MIC) influence reservoir performance and injection efficiency during EOR? ▾
Microbial-Induced Corrosion (MIC): bacteria (mainly SRB) corrode metal infrastructure while also damaging the near-wellbore zone.

Effects on EOR performance:
  • Reduced injectivity: biofilm + iron oxide deposits plug pore throats near injector wellbore.
  • Equipment failures: corroded tubing, casing, and surface pipelines → unplanned shutdowns.
  • H₂S production: "sours" the reservoir → more complex and expensive produced fluid handling (amine treatment, safety risks).
  • Reduced EOR agent contact: plugged wells = poor distribution of polymer/surfactant/steam.
  • Higher OPEX: biocide injection, corrosion monitoring, workovers add cost.
Lecture 11 · Reservoir Simulation and Modeling Principles
31 What is reservoir simulation, and how is it used to design and optimize EOR processes? ▾
Reservoir simulation: numerical (computer) modeling of fluid flow, pressure, and phase behavior in porous rock over time. Uses mathematical equations (Darcy's law, material balance, PVT equations).

Use in EOR design:
  • Predict performance before spending on field implementation.
  • Optimize injection rates, well locations, EOR agent concentration, and timing.
  • Compare scenarios: e.g., steam vs polymer vs CO₂ for a given reservoir.
  • History matching: calibrate model to actual production data → more reliable forecasts.
  • Risk reduction: identify problematic heterogeneity, souring risks, breakthrough timing before committing capital.
Common software: Eclipse (SLB), CMG STARS, INTERSECT, TEMPEST.

33 Given a heavy oil reservoir with high viscosity and shallow depth, propose a suitable EOR method and justify. ▾
Recommendation: Steam Flooding or SAGD (Steam-Assisted Gravity Drainage)

Justification:
  • Heavy oil + high viscosity → thermal EOR is the most effective way to reduce viscosity and improve flow.
  • Shallow depth → steam can be injected without excessive heat loss in tubing; economically viable pressure conditions.
  • For very thick, flat reservoirs: SAGD (horizontal wells) works best — steam rises, oil and condensate drain to lower producer well by gravity.
  • Real examples: Athabasca Oil Sands (Canada) — SAGD; Duri Field (Indonesia) — steam flooding.
Not recommended:
  • Chemical EOR — high cost, polymers may not work well in very heavy oil.
  • CO₂ — not miscible with very heavy oil at shallow depths.
34 How does well stimulation integrate with EOR to enhance production? ▾
Well stimulation: techniques to improve near-wellbore permeability — hydraulic fracturing (fracking), matrix acidizing.

Integration with EOR:
  • Better injectivity: fractures allow EOR fluids (steam, CO₂, polymer) to be injected at higher rates and penetrate deeper into the reservoir.
  • Connect unswept zones: fractures can bridge across tight barriers to reach by-passed oil.
  • Increase contact area: more of the EOR agent contacts the reservoir rock.
  • Acid stimulation: removes formation damage (scale, clay) before or during EOR to restore injectivity/productivity.
In unconventional EOR (shale oil), fracking is a prerequisite — without it, no EOR agent can enter the very-low-permeability rock.

36 What is the significance of reservoir boundary conditions in flow modeling? ▾
Boundary conditions tell the model what happens at the edges of the reservoir:
  • No-flow boundary: pressure declines as fluid is produced — acts like a sealed box. Common for fault-bounded reservoirs.
  • Constant pressure (aquifer) boundary: connected to water aquifer — pressure stays high even with production. Requires modeling aquifer influx.
Significance in flow modeling:
  • Wrong boundary → wrong pressure decline → wrong production forecast.
  • If aquifer support ignored: model over-predicts pressure decline → recommends unnecessary water injection.
  • If closed boundary assumed when aquifer is active: under-estimates natural energy, delays EOR unnecessarily.
  • Critical for well test interpretation (pressure transient analysis) — boundary responses appear in late-time pressure data.

38 What is formation damage, and how does it affect reservoir performance during EOR? ▾
Formation damage: reduction in reservoir permeability, especially near the wellbore, caused by physical, chemical, or biological processes.

Effects on EOR:
  • Reduced injectivity: plugged injector wells → can't maintain required injection rates → EOR process slows or fails.
  • Reduced productivity: plugged producer wells → less oil reaches surface per unit of effort.
  • Poor EOR agent distribution: if wellbore is damaged, steam, polymer, or CO₂ doesn't spread evenly.
  • Increased workovers: more frequent acid jobs, cleanouts → higher operating cost.
  • Quantified by skin factor (S) — a positive skin means damage; S = 0 is undamaged; negative S means stimulated.
39 List the main causes of formation damage in EOR processes. ▾
  • Fines migration: small particles detach from pore walls and block pore throats.
  • Clay swelling: freshwater injection causes smectite/illite to swell → pore blockage.
  • Scale precipitation: incompatible injected and formation waters react → CaCO₃, BaSO₄, FeSx deposits.
  • Asphaltene deposition: pressure/temperature or CO₂ mixing destabilizes asphaltenes → solid deposits.
  • Wax / paraffin deposition: cooling (e.g., cold water injection) precipitates wax from oil.
  • Bacterial plugging (biofouling): biofilm and bacterial products block pores.
  • Polymer retention: polymer flooding — excess polymer adsorbs on rock, reducing permeability.
  • Emulsion blocking: tight emulsions can block pores near wellbore.
40 Describe the environmental risks associated with large-scale EOR projects. ▾
  • CO₂ leakage (CO₂-EOR): unintended CO₂ escape into atmosphere or shallow groundwater aquifers.
  • Groundwater contamination: injected chemicals (polymers, surfactants, biocides) may leak into freshwater zones if well integrity fails.
  • Induced seismicity: high-pressure injection can reactivate faults (especially in CO₂ and wastewater injection).
  • H₂S emissions: reservoir souring from SRB produces toxic hydrogen sulfide gas.
  • Produced water disposal: large volumes of contaminated water need safe disposal.
  • Thermal effects: steam flooding changes subsurface temperature; potential effect on microecosystems.
  • Surface footprint: infrastructure, pipelines, roads disturb land and habitats.

43 Discuss the role of nanotechnology in improving modern EOR techniques. ▾
Nanoparticles (1–100 nm, e.g., SiO₂, Al₂O₃, TiO₂, carbon nanotubes) enhance EOR by:
  • Wettability alteration: nanoparticles adsorb on rock surface, shifting from oil-wet to water-wet → oil detaches more easily.
  • IFT reduction: lower interfacial tension between oil and injected fluid, mobilizing trapped oil.
  • Stabilizing foams and emulsions: nanoparticle-stabilized foams improve sweep by blocking high-k zones (like polymer).
  • Enhanced thermal flooding: nanoparticles improve heat transfer in steam flooding.
  • Nanosensors: injectable sensors to detect oil saturation and flow paths in the reservoir.
Still mostly lab/pilot stage. Key challenges: cost, nanoparticle aggregation in brine, and large-scale delivery into reservoir pores.
44 Explain how digital oilfield technologies (IoT sensors, real-time data) improve reservoir management and EOR decision-making. ▾
Digital Oilfield / Smart Field uses sensors, automation, and data analytics:
  • Real-time monitoring: downhole pressure, temperature, and flow rate sensors transmit data continuously → instant detection of problems.
  • Optimization without field visits: remote adjustment of injection/production rates based on data.
  • Digital twin: live reservoir simulation model updated with real-time data → more accurate forecasts and faster EOR decisions.
  • Predictive maintenance: ML algorithms predict equipment failures before they happen → reduce downtime.
  • Formation damage alerts: sudden injectivity drop detected in real time → biocide/acid treatment deployed quickly.
  • EOR agent optimization: adjust polymer concentration or steam rate in real time based on sensor feedback.
Result: higher production efficiency, lower OPEX, better EOR performance, and reduced environmental risk.
45 Application of machine learning in Geology — give software examples. ▾
ML in Geology:
  • Lithology/facies classification: train ML on labeled well logs → predict lithology in unlabeled wells.
  • Production forecasting: replace traditional decline curve analysis with neural networks.
  • Seismic attribute analysis: identify gas chimneys, DHIs (direct hydrocarbon indicators).
  • Petrophysical property prediction: porosity/permeability from seismic via ML.
  • Drilling optimization: predict optimal weight on bit, rotation speed.
Software examples:
  • Petrel + Blueback Toolbox — ML workflows in standard reservoir characterization
  • Python (scikit-learn, TensorFlow, PyTorch) — open-source; widely used in research and industry
  • DataRobot — automated ML platform used in oil companies
  • Paleoscan (Eliis) — ML-based seismic facies analysis
  • SAS Analytics — used for production analytics in NOCs
  • Ikon Science RokDoc — ML for rock physics inversion
